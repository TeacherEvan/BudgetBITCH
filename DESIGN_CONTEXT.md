## Design Context

### Users
BudgetBITCH is for average middle-class people trying to stay oriented when money feels noisy, fragmented, or unstable. They are likely opening the app during practical decision moments: checking where the pressure is, what needs attention first, and which next step will reduce stress fastest.

The first job to be done is fast financial orientation. The interface should help users understand their current situation quickly, identify the most useful next move, and avoid making them read or scroll through excess framing before they can act.

### Brand Personality
The existing product signals in the codebase point to a privacy-first, cinematic, story-aware budgeting product, but the preferred brand expression is tighter and more disciplined than theatrical. The voice should feel direct, calm, and capable.

Personality cues:
- privacy-first
- unsentimental
- concise

Emotional goal: replace money fog with immediate clarity and control, without bloated copy or over-explaining.

### Aesthetic Direction
The app should support both light and dark themes rather than staying dark-only. The visual direction should keep the product distinctive and editorial enough to feel intentional, but it must avoid bloat, unnecessary flourish, and long scrolling experiences.

References and anti-references derived from project context and user guidance:
- keep: strong hierarchy, dense-but-readable scanning, practical action-first surfaces
- avoid: overloaded explanations, decorative spectacle that delays action, layouts that force users to keep scrolling to find the point

The most important visual constraint is compression: every screen should get to the point fast, with minimal copy, explicit actions, and information arranged for immediate scanability.

### Design Principles
1. Lead with orientation. Show the user's situation, pressure points, and next action immediately.
2. Compress the interface. Remove bloated copy, repeated framing, and avoid scroll-heavy layouts whenever possible.
3. Make hierarchy do the work. Use typography, spacing, and grouping to create clarity before adding more containers or decoration.
4. Stay privacy-forward and trustworthy. The product should feel controlled, factual, and respectful rather than persuasive or noisy.
5. Design both themes intentionally. Light and dark modes should each feel native to the brand, not like one is a thin inversion of the other.
6. Keep interactions calm and fast. Motion, transitions, and progressive disclosure should clarify state, not perform personality.
